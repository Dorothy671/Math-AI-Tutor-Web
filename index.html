AI MATH TUTOR

Redefining the AI Math Tutor

AI MATH TUTOR is designed as an AI mathematics tutor that does not immediately reveal answers. Instead, it asks questions, offers prompts, and provides scaffolding so that learners can gradually arrive at mathematical understanding by themselves.

This project responds to a key educational challenge: when children get stuck at home, there is often no one available to guide their thinking in a timely and pedagogically effective way.

Rather than replacing teachers, the artefact fills the after-school learning gap by functioning as a Socratic-style digital tutor for young learners.

Educational Context and Problem

When a child gets stuck on mathematics homework at home, the available options are often limited, delayed, or pedagogically weak.

Ask Parents

Parents may have limited time, limited energy, or may have been away from formal schooling for too long to know how to teach effectively. When explanations are repeated but the child still does not understand, frustration can emerge on both sides.

Wait for Teachers

Children can wait until the next school day to ask a teacher. However, teachers work with many students and cannot realistically provide sustained one-to-one support for every learner after school. As a result, many moments of confusion are never addressed properly.

Search for Answers

Homework apps and search tools can instantly provide answers and worked solutions. Yet copying the answer often creates only an illusion of understanding. When a similar problem appears again, the learner still does not know how to reason through it independently.

Ask General AI

Current AI assistants are highly capable, but they are also highly answer-oriented. When learners can simply copy and paste a prompt to obtain a direct solution, the cognitive work of mathematical thinking is externalised rather than developed.

The core problem is not the lack of answers. The core problem is the lack of guided mathematical thinking.

Product Positioning

AI MATH TUTOR is positioned as an AI mathematics tutor rather than an answer engine.

It does not directly disclose solutions. It asks questions, gives hints, and provides scaffolding so that learners can move toward the answer through guided reasoning.

It is not intended to replace school teachers. Instead, it addresses the specific learning gap that emerges when a child is practising mathematics at home and lacks access to timely, individualised support.

The primary target users are elementary school students.

The main instructional focus is on elementary mathematical olympiad problems and other tasks that rely on logical reasoning, pattern recognition, and strategic thinking.

This focus is important because many elementary exercises are primarily computational. Those tasks offer relatively limited opportunities for guided conceptual dialogue. By contrast, olympiad-style problems require reasoning, structure, and explanation, which makes them especially suitable for AI-guided tutoring.

Target Users

Elementary school learners who need support with mathematical thinking rather than simple answer retrieval.

Teaching Scenarios

Olympiad mathematics, logical reasoning, finding patterns, combinational game problems, and other thinking-based mathematics tasks.

Educational Value

The product is designed not merely to improve task completion, but to support the development of explanation, reasoning, and transferable problem-solving habits.

Theoretical Foundation

AI MATH TUTOR is grounded in established educational theory. These theories are not simply written as fixed rules. Instead, they are organised into a knowledge base that informs the system’s tutoring behaviour dynamically.

Socratic Method — Collins & Stevens, 1982

The Socratic method emphasises guiding learners toward conclusions through structured questioning rather than direct explanation. In AI MATH TUTOR, when a learner states an answer such as “the second player wins,” the system does not merely mark it as correct. It follows with questions such as “If the opponent takes one piece, how many would you take?” and “Why would that move help you win?” In this way, the learner is guided to articulate reasoning, not just results.

The knowledge base stores questioning patterns, the use of counterexamples, and ways of surfacing hidden misconceptions.

Scaffolding — Wood, Bruner & Ross, 1976

Scaffolding theory suggests that support should be calibrated to the learner’s present understanding and gradually removed as competence stabilises. In this product, a learner may guess the correct answer before fully understanding it. The system therefore continues to support the learner at the level of strategy and pattern, only withdrawing support when understanding becomes more stable and demonstrable.

The knowledge base includes indicators of learner stage, conditions for increasing support, conditions for withdrawing support, and examples of appropriately challenging prompts.

Zone of Proximal Development — Vygotsky, 1978

The Zone of Proximal Development describes the space between what a learner can do independently and what can be accomplished with support. AI MATH TUTOR uses short-term, mid-term, and long-term memory to estimate this space dynamically. Based on the learner’s current state, it can decide whether more scaffolding is needed, whether support can be removed, or whether a different route of explanation is required.

The knowledge base includes methods for recognising the learner’s current zone, designing “just-beyond-current-level” challenges, and guiding efficiently within that zone.

Polya’s Problem Solving — Polya, 1945

Polya’s classic framework divides problem solving into four stages: understanding the problem, devising a plan, carrying out the plan, and looking back. In the product, when a learner becomes stuck, the system does not immediately reveal the method. Instead, it asks questions such as “What is the unknown?”, “What information is given?”, and “Have you seen a similar problem before?” This supports metacognition and transfer, not just local task completion.

The knowledge base therefore includes stage-based prompts and reflective questioning strategies.

In summary, code manages the procedural workflow, while the AI model determines how to teach within that workflow.

Core Mechanism

1. Three-Dimensional Mastery Tracking

Answer Correct

The learner is able to state the final conclusion required by the problem.

Strategy Correct

The learner can explain how the steps or moves work, not just what the final answer is.

Pattern Correct

The learner can identify and generalise the deeper mathematical rule or structure behind the problem.

This design is important because the system allows learners to guess the answer first, even uncertainly, but it does not treat that as full mastery. Learning is considered complete only when answer, strategy, and pattern are all demonstrated.

2. Three-Layer Memory and Dual-Driven Decision Making

Short-Term Memory (STM)

Records each turn within the current problem, including the learner’s answer state, independence, and clarity. This enables the system to understand where the learner is currently stuck.

Mid-Term Memory (MTM)

Tracks performance across a unit, aggregates mastery at the level of topic or question type, and supports decisions such as whether the learner should move forward, attempt a challenge task, or revisit a prerequisite idea.

Long-Term Memory (LTM)

Stores longer-term learner characteristics such as preferred learning style, tendency toward trial-and-error or caution, and transfer ability across sessions.

3. RAG Teaching Strategy Engine

AI MATH TUTOR does not rely solely on hardcoded logic. Instead, educational texts related to Socratic questioning, scaffolding, ZPD, and Polya’s approach are organised into a retrieval system. When the learner encounters difficulty, the system retrieves the most relevant pedagogical strategy and injects it into the prompt, allowing the model to respond more like an educator than a generic assistant.

Workflow: ReAct Decision Cycle

Thought → Action → Observation
1

Perception

The system receives learner input and reads the current sticking point, the learner’s unit progress, and longer-term learner characteristics.

2

Decision

The AI selects an action such as ask_question, give_hint, move_to_next, or go_back, depending on the learner’s current state.

3

Generation

The system produces an encouraging and pedagogically guided tutor response, informed by retrieved theory where appropriate.

4

Memory Update

Short-term, mid-term, and long-term memories are updated so that the next interaction is informed by prior performance.

Technical Implementation

Core Model

Large language models such as DeepSeek are used for reasoning-intensive decision making and structured JSON-style output.

Vector Database

ChromaDB is used to store and retrieve pedagogical strategy documents for retrieval-augmented generation.

Mastery Formula

Mastery = 0.4 × Independence + 0.4 × Clarity + 0.2 × Verification

Frontend Interaction

The current interface can be prototyped through a conversational tutoring interface, with future expansion into richer web-based environments.

Voice Support

Speech-to-text can be integrated to simulate a more natural tutor-like dialogue experience.

System Division of Labour

Code manages state, flow, memory, and progression logic; the AI model manages interpretation, action choice, and instructional dialogue generation.

AI Affordances: Why AI Agent Technology Can Address the Challenge

This section directly addresses the assignment’s emphasis on the affordances of digital technology.

Personalised Deep Guidance

AI agents make it possible to provide one-to-one instructional guidance at scale, something that is difficult to achieve through conventional after-school support.

Instant Feedback

Instead of waiting until the next day, learners can receive support during the crucial moment when confusion occurs, reducing the chance that misconceptions become fixed.

Dynamic Scaffolding

The level of support can be adjusted in real time according to the learner’s state. This creates a more responsive digital learning environment than static answer systems.

Teaching Thinking, Not Just Answers

Because the AI agent controls the release of information and the sequence of prompts, it can preserve the learner’s reasoning process rather than bypass it.

Approach Strength Limitation
Traditional Educational Apps Efficient and accessible Often shallow and answer-oriented
Human Tutors Deep and adaptive support Expensive and difficult to scale
AI Agent Tutor Deep + scalable + personalised guidance Requires deliberate pedagogical design and responsible implementation
In short, the affordance of AI Agent technology is not simply automation. Its educational value lies in responsive interaction, scalable personalisation, dynamic scaffolding, and the ability to support thinking rather than just solution retrieval.
